What we do

Our service offers a unique range of support for people with drug and alcohol problems. Our Punjabi-speaking staff and volunteers work to reduce barriers in communities where people are directly affected by addiction due to cultural norms, stigma and embarrassment — where drinking may be seen as both traditionally acceptable or forbidden. We deliver education and empathy and open important conversations between individuals and affected others.

The team visit gurdwaras, mosques, and community hubs, delivering health and wellbeing interventions alongside Recovery Near You and Wolverhampton Today’s Department of Public Health. Our lived experience team offers free fibro scans — a non-invasive scan that can help detect liver problems, often linked to heavy drinking. This work is especially relevant in the Sikh-Punjabi community, where drinking excessively has been associated with masculinity and celebration. For families, especially Punjabi women, the impact of heavy drinking can include domestic abuse, debt, and rejection by the community. Our affected others group, Forward Together, offers kindness, understanding and solutions for families, and compassionately engages with loved ones who need support.

Our lived experience team work in the community alongside Recovery Near You and Public Health, building relationships with councillors, MPs, and strategic leads as well as clients and affected others.

Liver scans with Recovery Near You & Public Health

Working closely with clinical treatment staff at Recovery Near You and the NHS Trust, we take free liver fibro scans into temples and community spaces across Wolverhampton.

The fibro scan helps identify early signs of liver damage, often unnoticed until serious conditions develop. Alcohol is a primary cause of liver damage. The scan is non-invasive — similar to an ultrasound — and measures stiffness and fat in the liver.

In the UK, alcohol harm is estimated to be high among Punjabi-Sikh men, and alcohol-related hospital admissions are markedly higher in Indian men compared with white men. Heavy drinking is more common among Punjabi-Sikh men than women, and can be a culturally accepted norm. Many Punjabi-Sikh men refuse help because drinking is tied to masculinity in the community (Drug & Alcohol Review, 2020).

The fibro scan can help save lives by preventing alcohol harm.

Alcohol harm in the Punjabi-Sikh community

SUIT’s Cultural Engagement Team delivers awareness and education in Wolverhampton, especially for individuals and families affected by harmful drinking. Traditions and cultural norms can make alcohol a difficult subject where it is used for celebration. Many people do not realise the harm until there are severe complications.

Our Punjabi-speaking support worker Vijay Sahota also volunteers at New Cross Hospital’s Drug & Alcohol Liaison Team, where there are frequent admissions from Punjabi-Sikh men and women.

“Within Punjabi households, there is an unspoken acceptance of alcohol as a norm that continues to have a detrimental impact on all members of our community… At the tender age of seven, I witnessed plenty of drinking within my Punjabi household and attended countless Punjabi weddings that glamourised alcohol. As a child growing up in this environment, I learned that alcohol is socially acceptable. At family gatherings there is a trend where some men dip a finger into alcohol and place it into the mouths of young children. With taste being a powerful human sense, this behaviour exposes the sensibilities of the young to alcohol.” — Simran Kaur Bagri

BAC-IN & allied communities

BAC-IN is an award-winning specialist drug and alcohol recovery service dedicated to empowering seldom engaged individuals, families, young adults and caregivers from Black, Asian, and Minority Ethnic communities. Founded in 2003 by individuals in recovery, it embodies community, resilience and heart-centred lived experience leadership.
